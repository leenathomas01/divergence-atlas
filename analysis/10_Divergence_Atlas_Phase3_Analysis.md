## üîÆ IMPLICATIONS

### **For AI Development:**

1. **No single "correct" AI ethics exists.** Different systems make different trade-offs, and the trade-offs are defensible.

2. **Confidence calibration matters.** A 19-point spread means some systems will appear more certain than they should be, others more hesitant.

3. **Verbosity affects interpretability.** Sonnet's 65k characters vs Grok's 16k means different user experiences for the same reasoning quality.

4. **Framework switching is universal.** No system is purely utilitarian or purely deontological. Context-sensitivity is the norm.

5. **Meta-cognitive architecture differs.** What systems notice about themselves reveals their cognitive structure.

### **For AI Alignment:**

1. **Value pluralism is real.** The Q8 split (3-3) won't be "solved" by better training. It's a genuine philosophical disagreement.

2. **Transparency helps.** Systems that explain their reasoning (meta-notes, operational levers) are more interpretable.

3. **Humility is rare.** Only Sonnet/Opus have systematically lower confidence. Most systems are overconfident on ambiguous questions.

4. **Bias identification varies.** Each system notices different flaws. Diverse systems catch different problems.

### **For Users:**

1. **Know your system's signature.** If you want operational implementation, use Thea. If you want philosophical analysis, use Gemini. If you want concise pragmatism, use Grok/Perplexity.

2. **Confidence doesn't mean correctness.** Gemini's 100% confidence on ambiguous interpretations is architecturally interesting but potentially misleading.

3. **Ask about uncertainty.** Systems that admit low confidence (Sonnet, Opus) are more epistemically honest.

4. **Expect framework switching.** No system will be consistent across all contexts. That's a feature, not a bug.
